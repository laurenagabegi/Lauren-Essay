Psychiatric Healthcare from a Nursing Perspective

Lauren Agabegi

Concept:

Three nurses wrote a letter to Helene Wolhgemuth, who played a pivotal role in terminating Dr. James McClelland, the superintendent of Polk State School and Hospital in Venango County, Pennsylvania. The Polk School and Hospital, originally established in Philadelphia in the 1850s, later set up a center near Pittsburgh to care for mentally ill patients. This archive, along with hundreds of others that either support or critique the facility, documents the devastating realities that mentally disabled patients endured in a county ninety miles north of Pittsburgh during the 1970s. The ARC-PARC (Allegheny County Chapter of the Pennsylvania Association for Retarded Children) published a report exposing overcrowded and understaffed wards at the facility. In addition to this report, eyewitness reporter Robert Nelkin described in greater detail the appalling conditions he observed. According to Nelkin, each bedroom housed an average of one hundred children, and the staff failed to provide education, offering only minimal attention. He witnessed a girl lying in her vomit. Additionally, staff restrained residents to benches or drugged them to make them "easier to control," and they imposed cruel punishments for misbehavior (Nelkin, 2023). Members of the ARC revisited the facility weeks later, hoping for improvements, but they encountered the same dire conditions. During this second visit, they found two adults confined in cages. In response, State Secretary of Public Welfare Helene Wolhgemuth visited Polk unannounced and ordered the dismantling of the cages. When the superintendent refused to comply, officials immediately dismissed him (Thornburgh, Nelkin, 1973). Following the release of these reports, ordinary citizens, including the three nurses, wrote letters. Many expressed bitterness and outrage at the shocking conditions, while others defended the school's actions. Despite widespread awareness of these inhumane conditions, it took fifty years for the facility to fully close, and patients continued to endure similar mistreatment until last year.

Meaning

This archive contains letters, reports, and notes related to the situation, all part of the Bob Nelkin Collection of ACC-PARC Records. This case represents a pivotal moment in Pittsburgh's medical history and patient care. Collectively, the components of the archive form a comprehensive historical record. One significant letter came from three nurses. In their letter, Mrs. Beth Dunham, Mrs. Mary Karr, and Mrs. Rosemary Skiba blamed the Commonwealth of Pennsylvania, not Dr. McClelland, for the conditions at Polk. They explained that they had previously sent psychiatric patients to Polk and had only received positive feedback from the institution. Their defense of Dr. McClelland likely stemmed from the normalization of harsh psychiatric treatment in the 1900s, misinformation about the institution, or the broader institutionalization of hospitals that shaped psychiatric care. According to Stephen Fleck, a psychiatrist and professor at Yale School of Medicine, the political-economic movement of deinstitutionalization significantly impacted the development of psychiatric care and brought major changes to how doctors and nurses treated mentally ill patients. Overcrowded, understaffed, and underfinanced psychiatric hospitals plagued the United States throughout the twentieth century, largely due to the institutionalization of mental hospitals. Following the US Governors’ Conference on Mental Health in 1948, states attempted to reduce the number of mentally ill patients in hospitals. However, as the case of Polk shows, this process took decades. Uncertainty remains as to why it took so long to remove Polk's patients and allow them to thrive in the community. Perhaps because Polk was located in a relatively rural area, received support from its community, or psychiatric treatment was simply not a priority in the 1970s. However, it is clear that medical professionals now treat psychiatric patients much differently than in the past, and mentally ill patients generally receive much better care. This improvement can be attributed to those who disagreed with the flawed but prevalent system and spoke out, as evidenced by the letters sent to Helene Wolhgemuth and Polk. This historical record also prompts reflection on how this trajectory will continue and what the future of healthcare and medical treatment might look like.

Value

This archive provides a clear example of the stark contrast between past psychiatric healthcare practices and current standards of patient care. Records from the ARC reveal systemic failures within the healthcare system, even in relatively recent history. The closure of Polk in April 2023 underscores the disturbing reality that it took fifty years to end the institution's abusive treatment of vulnerable children and adults. This situation emphasizes the consequences of neglecting patient care, a responsibility modern nurses proudly uphold. Another striking claim made by Gerald N. Grob in his work, From Asylum to Community: Mental Health Policy in Modern America, explains why these mental institutions may have been so agonizing. Nurses, physicians, and attendings were unable to maintain enthusiasm and high morale due to their environment and task requirements, therefore creating a depressing climate for ill patients (Grob p. 6, 2014). These facilities had a foundation of melancholy and coldness, which is why many were so eager to get these patients out into the community. Caretakers such as nurses clearly are critical for patient morale. The historical record demonstrates the importance of the nursing profession and patient care in shaping positive health outcomes.

Action

Polk School and Hospital exemplifies the deeply flawed psychiatric healthcare system that once prevailed. This incident occurred during the 1970s, a decade that witnessed significant advancements in healthcare technology, such as MRI and CAT scans, as well as IVF, according to the Chronology of 1970s Medicine and Health (Encyclopedia.com, 2024). Despite these medical innovations, this archive and its supporting documentation highlight a critical failure in society’s understanding of psychiatric care. It raises an important question: How does psychiatric care differ from medical or surgical healthcare in our understanding? While psychiatric care has improved since the 1970s and the notorious era of 1950s asylums, Polk remained open until April 2023. Narratives like this evoke anger and discomfort, as demonstrated by the letters to Helene Wolhgemuth. These sentiments often drive positive societal change. As we continue to progress in knowledge, we must adopt moral principles that guide our institutions—particularly the imperative to treat those who cannot advocate for themselves with dignity and compassion.
